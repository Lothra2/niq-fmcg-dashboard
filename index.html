Switzerland pays $9.08 for what Taiwan prices at $2.47 — the gap widened sharply in 2025 as the Swiss franc and European currencies surged vs. the dollar. The US baseline itself rose from $5.79 to $6.12. Any FMCG brand running a "global price floor" is leaving volume on the table in 25+ markets.

02
What FMCG Brands Must Do
Sachets are access. Multi-packs are loyalty. Price floors are the enemy of volume.
  • 📦India & Indonesia ($2.51–$2.52 Big Mac): Sub-$3 Big Mac signals any SKU above $1 needs a value story or smaller pack. Sachets in personal care drive 60%+ of category volume. This isn't a compromise — it's the category.
  • 🇲🇽Mexico now above US ($6.17 vs $6.12): Highest per-capita Coke consumption globally. BM crossing US parity is a signal — the Mexican consumer is richer than the price signals suggest. Premium SKUs have untapped headroom here.
  • 🇹🇼Taiwan paradox ($85K GDP PPP, $2.47 Big Mac): World's cheapest Big Mac among high-income economies. Premium product lines have enormous untapped headroom. The consumer can afford it — the market just hasn't been offered it right.
  • 🟢Argentina ($5.53 — down from $7.40): The biggest pricing story of 2025. Inflation fell from 60% to 16.4% under Milei reforms. Private label still dominant, but the path back to branded premium is opening. Watch this market.
02 · Inflation Playbook
Three Inflation Regimes. Three Completely Different FMCG Strategies.
The biggest mistake: one strategy across different inflation regimes. The map changed in 2025 — Argentina dramatically cut inflation, China is exiting deflation, and Turkey now leads the hyperinflation table. Update your playbook accordingly.
🔴 Hyperinflation (>20%) — Survival Mode
Consumers shop every 2–3 days instead of weekly. Basket size shrinks. Price per unit matters more than pack size. Local brands outperform multinationals by 15–25pts. Private label is the only growth vector. Note: Argentina has exited this zone (now 16.4%).
🇹🇷 Turkey 24.7%🇳🇬 Nigeria 17%
🟢 Stable (<3%) — Premiumization Window
Volume recovery is real. Brand equity investments pay off. Premiumization unlocked in health, wellness, and convenience. Price-pack moves are about growth architecture, not crisis management.
🇩🇪 Germany 2.1%🇯🇵 Japan 2.7%🇫🇷 France 0.9%🇻🇳 Vietnam 3.2%🇨🇷 Costa Rica 1.0%
🟡 Moderate (3–15%) — Value Architecture War
The most contested space. Private label vs. national brand battles intensify. Promotional ROI under scrutiny from retailers. Value tiers gain share. Argentina (16.4%) is entering this zone for the first time since 2021.
🇬🇧 UK 2.8%🇪🇬 Egypt 13.5%🇵🇰 Pakistan 7%🇦🇷 Argentina 16.4%
🔵 Deflation (≤0%) — Value Perception Crisis
China is exiting deflation — CPI now +0.2%, the first positive reading in 12 months. Stimulus and policy bank lending are working. The deflationary FMCG environment is not over, but the floor may be in. Monitor closely.

04
The Four Digital Realities in FMCG
Same metric, four completely different strategic implications.
  • ⚡India's Quick-Commerce: Blinkit & Zepto deliver grocery in <10 min. SKU assortment follows "emergency restock" logic, not planogram logic. Brands that win here own the impulse moment, not the weekly shop.
  • 📱SEA Social Commerce: Vietnam, Indonesia, Malaysia are buying via TikTok Shop and Instagram. Discovery = purchase in one tap. Influencer ROI here beats traditional trade investment 3:1 in some categories.
  • 🏪LATAM Super-App Model: Rappi in Colombia/Mexico has become grocery, restaurant, and pharmacy in one. For FMCG, this is a new retail partner — not just a delivery channel.

04 · Demographics
Two FMCG Worlds. One Product Catalogue. That's the Problem.
A 31-year median age gap separates the youngest market (Nigeria, 18) from the oldest (Japan, 49). The same multinational's portfolio is trying to serve toddler parents in Lagos and retirees in Osaka. Not with different products — with the same ones.
🔴 Young Populations — Volume & Access Markets
  • 🇳🇬Nigeria (18 yrs, 230M pop): Largest young population in Africa. Snacking, beverages, and personal care lead. Sachet economy is the category. Mobile-first, informal retail dominant.
  • 🇮🇳India (28 yrs, 1.44B pop): World's most important FMCG growth engine. Young consumer + rising income + digital penetration = the rarest combination in modern FMCG history.
  • 🇵🇭Philippines (25 yrs, 118M pop): BPO-driven income growth creating new middle-class consumers fast. Sari-sari stores still dominant but organized retail converting rapidly.
🔵 Aging Populations — Health & Convenience Markets
  • 🇯🇵Japan (49 yrs, 124M pop): Super-aged society. 14,000+ 7-Eleven stores because aging consumers shop small, shop often. Health + functionality labels drive 40% category premium. Portion size is the R&D brief.
  • 🇩🇪Germany (45 yrs, 84M pop): Sustainability is a purchase criterion, not an aspiration. Aldi/Lidl discipline means value must be proven, not claimed. Private label share exceeds 40% in most FMCG categories.
  • 🇰🇷South Korea (45 yrs, 52M pop): K-beauty and health premiumization is both domestic and export-led. Coupang's same-day delivery is the baseline. Aging consumers here spend more, not less.

Across every income level and every region, value-engineered retail is winning. This isn't because consumers are "poor" — it's because they've recalibrated what value means after years of inflation and pricing games.

06
Who's Winning — Market by Market
Five distinct discount models. Each requires a different response from FMCG brands.
  • 🇩🇪Aldi/Lidl (WE): Reframed quality expectations globally. Now expanding in US + Australia. Their private label sets the price floor every national brand must justify against.

  • 🇨🇴D1/ARA (Colombia): Hard discount grew from 0 to 40%+ in 10 years. FMCG multinationals lost distribution access. The lesson: win in hard discount or lose relevance in the store.

What This Report Is — and Isn't
Purpose & Scope

Purpose

This dashboard compiles publicly available macro-economic, demographic, and retail indicators across 42 markets into a single view for FMCG and retail strategic planning. It is designed for internal NielsenIQ teams — analytics, customer success, commercial, and leadership — to quickly benchmark markets, spot structural patterns, and frame client conversations.

Think of it as a macro context layer, not a primary research tool. The data should inform judgment, not replace it. Last updated: March 2026. This version uses The Economist Big Mac Index (January 2026), IMF WEO October 2025 + January 2026 Update, and UN demographic data (WPP 2024, WUP 2025).

What It Is Not

This report is not a primary NIQ retail measurement study. It does not use NIQ panel data, POS data, or proprietary consumer surveys. The e-commerce penetration figures, Coca-Cola prices, and qualitative market signals are estimates from public third-party sources — they are directionally useful but should not be cited as precise figures in external client presentations without additional validation.

For external use, please add the footnote provided at the bottom of this page. Data currency: Big Mac prices are from January 2026; macro indicators from IMF WEO October 2025 + January 2026 Update.

FMCG Market Signal — Definitions & Classification Criteria
Qualitative Labels · Data Explorer Column

How Market Signals Are Assigned

The Market Signal is a qualitative label synthesized from the 11 quantitative indicators in this dataset. It is not a published NIQ metric, not an official classification, and not derived from a scoring algorithm. It represents analyst judgment applied consistently across markets to produce a one-line strategic positioning statement.

Each signal is assigned by evaluating a primary trigger condition (the dominant characteristic of the market) and one or more secondary conditions that confirm the classification. The criteria below are the explicit rules used. When a market sits on the boundary between two signals, the primary trigger takes precedence.

Internal use only. Do not cite as a NielsenIQ proprietary classification in external client presentations. Use the label as a conversation frame, not a deliverable.

Pack size optimization toward smaller, higher-value formats. 🇺🇸 US · 🇨🇦 Canada · 🇫🇷 France · 🇦🇺 Australia · 🇨🇭 Switzerland · 🇮🇹 Italy
💻 Mature / Digital GDP PPP > $55K AND e-commerce ≥ 25% Inflation < 3% AND physical retail stable but digital is the growth vector. Same-day or next-day delivery is consumer baseline expectation. Omnichannel is non-negotiable. Digital shelf, search, and ratings are as important as physical placement. DTC and platform strategies must be market-specific. 🇬🇧 UK · 🇳🇱 Netherlands · 🇰🇷 South Korea · 🇸🇪 Sweden · 🇹🇼 Taiwan
🕰 Mature / Aging · Slow Median age > 45 AND GDP growth < 1% GDP PPP > $50K AND population declining or stagnant. Structural demand shift toward health, functionality, convenience, and smaller portion sizes. Demographic-driven portfolio shift. Functional and health claims drive premium. Convenience formats (konbini, small-store) gaining vs. hypermarkets. Category innovation is the growth lever. 🇯🇵 Japan · 🇮🇹 Italy · 🇩🇪 Germany
💎 Premium Niche / City-State GDP PPP > $90K AND population < 15M Inflation < 2.5% AND urbanization > 70%. Extremely high income concentrated in a small geography. Price anchors for the broader region — what sells here at premium sets aspirational ceiling for neighbors. Global premium benchmark market. Not a volume play. Use for brand credibility, pricing reference, and test-and-learn for premium innovations before broader rollout. 🇸🇬 Singapore ($161K) · 🇨🇭 Switzerland ($97K)
🔥 Inflation Distorted CPI inflation > 15% All other KPIs are distorted. Big Mac price in USD may look high (currency devaluation) or basket data may look stable (substitution effects). GDP growth in real terms is compressed. Consumer behavior driven by price frequency, not value. Survival mode for branded FMCG. Local brands and private label win. Basket fragmentation, smaller pack sizes, higher trip frequency. Promotional ROI collapses. Invest in brand equity but manage working capital tightly. 🇹🇷 Turkey (24.7%) · 🇳🇬 Nigeria (17%)
📉→📈 Recovery / Inflation CPI inflation 10–20% declining from a higher peak AND GDP growth turning positive Inflation trending down year-on-year AND currency stabilizing. Consumer behavior still cautious but improving. Private label at peak share — branded recovery possible within 12–18 months. Watch market. The window to reintroduce branded premium is opening. First movers in value-tier rebuild will capture share. Do not exit — reposition. 🇦🇷 Argentina (16.4%, down from 60%) · 🇪🇬 Egypt (13.5%)
❄️ Deflationary + Digital CPI inflation ≤ 0.5% AND e-commerce > 35% GDP growth still positive but consumer sentiment deflationary (purchase deferral behavior). Discount platforms dominating. Quality narrative the only way to justify price in this environment. Do not compete on price — the floor is always lower. Invest in quality differentiation, provenance, and brand trust. Sam's Club and premium fresh formats show the viable alternative. 🇨🇳 China (0.2% CPI, 42% e-comm)
🌐 Frontier / High Potential GDP PPP < $10K AND population > 50M Modern trade penetration < 30% AND informal retail dominant. E-commerce typically < 10%. Long-run volume potential significant but requires non-standard distribution (sachet economy, mobile payments, informal trade partnerships). Volume play, not margin play. Sachet formats unlock access. Mobile payment infrastructure is the e-commerce enabler. Patience required — 5–10 year horizon for meaningful return on modern trade investment. 🇳🇬 Nigeria · 🇰🇪 Kenya · 🇵🇰 Pakistan
⚖️ Dual Economy Unemployment > 25% AND GDP PPP < $20K Extreme inequality (top income segment vs. mass market). Two entirely different FMCG markets coexist within the same country — premium modern trade for high earners, sachet economy for the majority. Single portfolio strategy does not work. Requires a two-portfolio approach. Premium imported brands for top-income urban consumers. Sachet, local manufacturing, and price-locked SKUs for mass market. Route-to-market must serve both simultaneously. 🇿🇦 South Africa (32.8% unemploy.)
⚓ Stable / Strategic GDP growth 1–3% AND inflation < 5% Modern trade established AND no dominant structural disruption (not hyperinflation, not deflation, not frontier). Market performing within expected range. Predictable consumer behavior. Strategic importance often exceeds economic size (e.g. geographic hub, cultural influence). Maintain and defend. Promotional efficiency and shelf execution matter more than market development spend. Trade partnership quality is the primary competitive lever. 🇲🇽 Mexico · 🇨🇱 Chile · 🇵🇪 Peru · 🇨🇷 Costa Rica · 🇬🇹 Guatemala
Important: How to Apply These Criteria
Primary trigger wins
When a market qualifies for multiple signals, the most structurally dominant condition takes precedence. Argentina qualifies as both Recovery and High Growth — Recovery wins because inflation is still the dominant consumer behavior driver.
Signals are point-in-time
Markets move between signals as indicators change. Argentina moved from Inflation Distorted (2024) to Recovery (2025) in 12 months. China may move from Deflationary to Stable as CPI normalizes. Update classification with each data refresh.
Internal framing only
These labels are an internal thinking tool. Do not present them in client deliverables as a NielsenIQ methodology without additional documentation. If a client asks how signals are defined, this criteria table is the reference to share.

⚠️ Transparency Note on E-Commerce Data

There is no single global official database that publishes "FMCG e-commerce as % of grocery retail" across all 42 markets using a consistent methodology. Different providers (Euromonitor, eMarketer, Kantar, Statista) measure different things: some cover online grocery only, others include food delivery, others use different base years. The e-commerce figures in this report are estimates compiled from multiple industry sources and should be treated as directional benchmarks, not precise measurements. The most reliable figures are for China, UK, US, South Korea, Japan, and Australia — where multiple independent sources align. The least reliable are for Sub-Saharan Africa, Central America, and Pakistan, where published data is very limited.

✅ Verified
The Economist — Big Mac Index
economist.com/big-mac-index
Covers: Big Mac Price (USD), Big Mac Index vs. US (%). Published January 2026. US baseline: $6.12 (up from $5.79 in Jan 2025). The index is calculated as: (local Big Mac price in USD / $6.12) − 1. Raw data available on GitHub: TheEconomist/big-mac-data. Covers 54 countries.
This is the most commonly cited informal purchasing-power-parity measure in economics journalism. Consistent methodology since 1986.
✅ Verified
IMF World Economic Outlook
imf.org/en/publications/weo/issues/2025/10/14/weo-october-2025
imf.org/en/publications/weo/issues/2026/01/19/weo-update-january-2026
Covers: GDP per Capita PPP (NGDPdpc), Real GDP Growth % (NGDP_RPCH), CPI Inflation % (PCPIPCH), Unemployment Rate (LUR). Download the WEO database directly from the IMF website and filter by country and indicator code to verify any figure.
The October 2025 WEO is the most complete country-level release (full statistical appendix). The January 2026 Update revised India growth to 7.3%, China to 5.0%, Argentina to +4.0%. GDP growth figures are IMF projections, not actuals — the next full release is April 2026.
✅ Verified
United Nations — WPP & WUP
population.un.org/wpp · population.un.org/wup
Covers: Population (UN World Population Prospects 2024), Median Age (UN WPP 2024), Urbanization Rate % (UN World Urbanization Prospects 2025). These are 2025 projections based on census data and demographic modeling.
UN demographic projections are the global standard used by governments, the World Bank, and academic institutions. Methodology is consistent across all 42 markets.
✅ Verified
World Bank — National Accounts
data.worldbank.org/indicator/NE.CON.PRVT.ZS
Covers: Private Consumption as % of GDP (NE.CON.PRVT.ZS). Note: most recent actuals are 2023 data — 2025 values reflect the last available year, not a forecast. Use indicator code to verify each country.
Private consumption data typically lags 1–2 years. The 2023 figures are used as a structural proxy — this metric changes slowly and the lag does not materially affect strategic interpretation.
✅ Verified
ILO — ILOSTAT
ilostat.ilo.org · ILO modelled estimates
Covers: Unemployment Rate (%). ILO uses a harmonized methodology across all countries, which makes cross-market comparisons valid. This is preferred over national statistics bureaus for consistency. Note: some markets use national definitions that differ from ILO harmonized rates.
ILO modelled unemployment estimates use a common framework across countries — critical for valid comparisons between, for example, Nigeria and Germany.
⚠️ Estimate
Numbeo & Expatistan
numbeo.com · expatistan.com
Covers: Coca-Cola 2L Price (USD). Crowdsourced cost-of-living platforms. Users in each country submit local retail prices, which are averaged. Methodology is not audited and prices vary significantly within a country (urban vs. rural, supermarket vs. convenience store).
Treat Coke prices as approximate illustrations (±15–20% margin). For precise retail pricing, NIQ's own measurement data per market would be the authoritative source.

euromonitor.com · emarketer.com · various national reports
